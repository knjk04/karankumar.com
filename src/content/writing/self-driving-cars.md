Self-driving cars, or autonomous vehicles (AV) more generally, used to scare me. 
They stood out to me as the clearest example of AI taking over people's jobs. 
There are millions of drivers worldwide. 
Taking over their jobs just because we can felt unfair.
However, I've made a U-turn after reading '21 Lessons for the 21st
Century' by Yuval Noah Harari, who claims that self-driving
cars are a moral imperative.

## Job losses

Lots of people make a living by driving vehicles: cab/taxi drivers,
HGV (heavy goods vehicles like lorries and trucks) drivers,
bus drivers, chauffeurs, delivery drivers (e.g. Amazon, DPD, UPS, etc.), 'man/woman in a van' drivers and more.
By automating their jobs, we are taking away their source of income.

Some argue that we shouldn't stand in the way of technological progress.
Those drivers can be retrained to do other types of work that is
still needed in a modern economy. One thorny question is who pays for this
retraining. If you say it's the government, then 
we may need to take a closer look at how effective the existing
strategies are for finding work in your country. At least in the UK,
I have severe doubts. You may argue that the companies that benefit
from the AVs (Uber (or similar), the AV makers, etc.) should pay more in tax to compensate for this.
In the absence of regulation to require this, it's hard to know
whether those companies would voluntarily commit to this. You might say those people whose jobs will be automated are responsible.
Automation will likely come in waves rather than all at once, so
there could be time to adapt. While this sounds promising, it may
be challenging for these drivers to actually adapt. Many Uber drivers in the UK, for example, often work long hours to provide
for their families, so taking time out of that may be difficult.
And then what would they retrain in? What if they end up retraining
in a field that is next to be automated? I think there should be
some guidance for those affected on what the market needs most
and where they can best use their skills.

Recently, there are numerous reports in Europe of a shortage of HGV drivers. It's a similar case in the U.S. Fewer young people
want to take up a career in driving large vehicles, perhaps out of
concern for the limited future career prospects.
However, until we have fully autonomous vehicles, that's a gap
that needs to be filled.
To increase the number of active HGV drivers, the UK government
recently announced a plan for its Department of Education to invest [£10 million into training programmes](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/more-support-to-help-people-to-become-hgv-drivers-among-package-of-government-measures-to-ease-risk-of-shortages) to get up to 3,000 more HGV
drivers on the road. They're also pleading with those who have
HGV vehicles to return. If similar schemes can be implemented
in other areas where supply is critical, then assuming the programmes
are effective, this could be a useful solution for helping
displaced employees pivot their career.

## Saving lives

Those in favour of self-driving cars often cite figures like
38,000 deaths on U.S. roads or 1.19 million road deaths globally<sup>1</sup>.

Humans may not be the best drivers. Even if we exclude the reckless
drivers, speeders, those that drive under the influence of alcohol
or drugs, tailgaters and all the other drivers that are intentionally
bad or annoying, our roads could still be a lot safer
(ask any cyclist).

Drivers that unintentionally fall asleep, even if it's micro-sleep
for a couple of seconds, could risk their lives and those of others.
Eliminating daylight saving (the most dangerous day to be on the road)
and having controls in cars that can
detect signs of drowsiness could be effective.

Still, accidents happen. The promise with self-driving cars is that
they can react more quickly than we humans can (e.g. brake sooner),
won't have blind spots, won't get tired or distracted, will
drive better in poor weather conditions (e.g. darkness and fog) and
won't drive irresponsibly.

While they may be safer than humans today, there are unfortunately
still tragic casualties. News reports of incidents where mistakes
were made that wouldn't have fooled most drivers, 
like [mistaking a cyclist for an unknown object](https://www.wired.com/story/uber-self-driving-crash-arizona-ntsb-report/), are unacceptable. Our safety bar
is rightfully considerably higher for autonomous vehicles. It may
be hard for today's self-driving cars to handle strange edge
cases they had never previously encountered, but it's a technical
challenge we need to solve and prove before we get widespread
acceptance.

I previously mentioned that autonomy could come in waves. Here
are the different levels of autonomy:

**Level 0: no automation**

A car that doesn't have any autonomous technology, such as sensors
and cameras. These are the most popular types of cars today.

**Level 1: driver assistance**

Vehicles that can assist drivers, but a trained driver is still
needed behind the wheel.

Modern vehicles are level 1 if they have features like cruise 
control and help keep you in your lane.

**Level 2: partial automation**

A human driver still has to keep their hands on the wheel at all 
times. The car can help with managing speed and steering, ensuring
you maintain a safe distance from the car in front.

An example is [Tesla's Autopilot](https://www.tesla.com/en_gb/support/autopilot). 

**Level 3: conditional automation**

Level 3 vehicles can drive themselves under ideal conditions, 
such as when on a motorway/highway where there are clear
markings dividing the separate lanes.

You don't need to have your hands on the steering wheel, but a 
qualified driver is still required to sit in the driving seat
to take over if needed.

**Level 4: high automation**

Level 4 vehicles will be able to drive themselves without human
input in most scenarios. This may be limited to known cases
where the level 4 vehicles have demonstrated that they're safe.
Had there not been legal restrictions in place, we likely
would have seen more level 4 vehicles on the road today.

**Level 5: full automation**

True driverless vehicles. They should be able to handle all
scenarios and not need any human intervention. In theory, you
could put a child, a blind person or an unqualified driver
in the driver's seat.

See <sup>2</sup> for the source of these five levels.

As humans drive less and trust autonomous vehicles more,
our skills could atrophy (particularly for level 4+).
This may mean that if you are needed to intervene in a 
hazardous situation, you may no longer be sufficiently
skilled to handle the situation effectively due to lack
of practice.

It's purely speculative, but I wonder if we'll reach a point
where we get a temporary spike in the number of road accidents
when more vehicles on the road are autonomous. The hope is that
these issues will be quickly patched by the AV 
makers, so they get better over time.

## A moral imperative

Coming back to Yuval Noah Harari, he posed a simple question in his
book. What matters more: saving jobs or saving lives? 
While we ideally want both, if self-driving cars are shown
to make roads safer, it would be remiss not to expand adoption.
It's worth mentioning that Tesla's Autopilot is a good example
of a middle ground; it can warn human drivers if it detects
a potential collision, but humans are still in charge.

That being said, it can be hard to justify autonomous vehicles if
flaws in the autonomous systems claim lives, especially for the families affected.
My hope is that autonomous vehicles will follow a similar path
to aeroplanes, where safety improves as technology improves, and 
necessary regulation is put in place to demand a very high
safety standard.

If we can make autonomous driving safe, I'd agree with Harari that
it's a moral imperative, and saving lives takes precedence over saving jobs.
